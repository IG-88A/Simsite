Basic Needs For Your PC

When building or picking a PC (if your go the pre-built route), the most powerful doesn't always fit your needs or if youre on a budget. Titles like ACC are pretty demanding if you use VR for instance, but titles like iRacing and rFactor and RaceRoom usually arent in VR. The big foucs in my opinion is a smooth and steady frame rate.

  • What other uses does your PC have?

    • Will you just be using it just for sim racing? Will you be playing other games? Do you want to edit photo and video? This are big factors whe it comes to selecting parts for your PC. Unless you are editing photos and video or use some of the more modern flight sims, you do not need more than 16GB.

  • What display method are you using? Are you going single screen? VR? Triple Monitor? Wide Screen?

    • My current PC uses a 8GB 1070Ti and runs most VR games and titles reasonably well. One of my last sim builds I had a customer that wanted triple screens on a budget and using iRacing only (iRacing is not demanding by design) and gets 90-100 FPS on a Nvidia 1650.

  • Build a balanced system

    • If you have the budget to build an rocket ship then this wont apply to you, I am sure you will already be getting the best on the current market (I'm totally not jealous) Building a balacnced system means parity between your graphics card and CPU. Having something like an i9 CPU paired with a (lower tierd GPU) wont mean you have a fast PC. Instead of getting an i9, aim for a i7 or K series i5. Free up your budget to buy a better card. A great resource I use to check realworld performance is GPU Benchmark. You can more or less build your PC and youll probably find some one with performance numbers already!

Cockpits

Think of a cockpit as a part of the foundation of your house. Your PC is the concrete slab and your cockpit is the walls. Before you go and buy the most expensive cockpit think about your gear that you want use or eventually use (pedals and wheel). While a cockpit made out of wood or PVC pipes is great for a gear driven wheel (Logitechs and the intro Thrustmaster wheel bases). On the other hand if you purchase a Direct Drive wheel, do not expect a long life out of DIY kit.

For example, I have a Next Level Racing GT Track, while it is the top of line model for Next Level Racing, I can tell there is a tiny amount of flex on the pedal plate using my load cell pedals. Is that a deal breaker for me? No. Its minimal and I feel like I can easily get the most out of my loadcell pedals. But, if I were to upgrade to say a set of pedals like HPPs or maybe set based off real life pedals. I may have issues. For the time being, I wont be upgrading my cockpit.

Do research on what works best for you and the gear you want. If your want to put in the effort and homework. Design your own 80/20 rig buy using (MAY CAD) or (80/20.net)

If you need plans check out (Open Sim Racing) or check out cockpit brands that I have had great luck with as well as other sim racers

  • Sim Lab
  • Next Level Racing


If you love adventure you do like many people have done and use CAD software to design your own extruded aluminum (80/20) cockpit. And of course you will need to get your own seat. While you can get a purpose build racing bucket seat, main people love going to their local auto salvage and pulling from a junk car (for ALOT less money).

Pedals

Out side of a good PC and cockpit that fits your needs. The only thing that will truly make you faster is a pedals. What your feet do is just as important as what your hands do. Having a pedal set with a loadcell brake and high resolution clutch and throttle pedals is what should get straight out of the box.

In my opinion the best set of pedals available at the moment is the Thrustmaster T-LCMs. They are currently around the $199 USD mark and have a 100kg loadcell for the brake and 16k resolution for both throttle and clutch.
